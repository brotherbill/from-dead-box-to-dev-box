# Can I Do This?  
### Purpose: Provide calm reassurance for parents who feel unsure about doing a real install.  
###### C:/dev/repos/from-dead-box-to-dev-box/guided-assembly/00-can-i-do-this.md

It is completely normal to feel nervous about installing an operating system.  
Most parents have never done it.  
Most people who use computers every day have never done it.  
Feeling unsure does not mean you are unqualified — it means you care.

This guide was written **for people who have never done anything like this before**.  
Every step is calm, simple, and intentional, and then adds a full learning system, with configuration steps.  
You will not be asked to do anything unsafe, risky, or technical.

---

## Why This Feels Scary

Computers have trained people to believe:

- “If I click the wrong thing, I might break it.”  
- “I don’t know what any of this means.”  
- “This looks like something only experts should touch.”  

Those feelings are real.  
But the truth is simpler:

> A fresh install is just a sequence of small, safe steps.  
> You don’t need to understand the computer — you only need to follow the steps.

---

## Why You *Can* Do This

This path was intentionally designed for non‑technical parents.  
It follows three principles:

1. **No surprises**  
1. **No jargon**  
1. **No decisions you have to make alone**

You will always know:

- what you are doing  
- why you are doing it  
- what the screen should look like  
- what the next step is  

If something unexpected appears, the guide tells you exactly what to do.

---

## Watching the Videos First

Along with the written steps, there are short videos that show each step being done on a real computer.  
You can:

- read the step  
- watch the video  
- then do the step yourself  

This gives you the reassurance that you have already seen someone do it safely.

### Watching “scary” steps more than once

Some steps may feel unfamiliar at first.  
That is normal.

If a step feels scary, you can:

- watch the video again  
- and again  
- and again  

Each time you watch it, the fear goes down a little.  
After a few repetitions, the step stops being scary and becomes **routine**.

And routine steps are easy to follow.

This is the same method the Apollo astronauts used in their simulators:  
they practiced the same sequence until it felt simple and obvious.

You can do the same thing here.

---

## One Task at a Time, One Step at a Time

You can do this the same way the astronauts did it:

> **One task at a time.  
> One step at a time.  
> Calm.  
> No rush.**

There is no timer.  
There is no pressure.  
There is no “falling behind.”

You move at your pace, calmly and steadily.

---

## UOW Boundaries Keep You Safe

Each part of this guide is broken into small Units of Work (UOW).  
A UOW is a short, self‑contained task that you can complete in one sitting.

You will always know:

- when a UOW starts  
- when it ends  
- how long it takes  
- what “done” looks like  

This lets you commit the right amount of time before you begin.

### UOWs Are Interruptible

Life happens.  
Children need attention.  
Doorbells ring.  
Phones buzz.  
Dinner is ready.  
Fire alarms go off.

A UOW is designed so you can **stop immediately**, take care of real life, and come back later.

When you return, you simply start that UOW from the top of the page.  
No guessing.  
No confusion.  
No “Where was I?”

### What a UOW Does *Not* Mean

A UOW never means:

- “This must be finished right now.”  
- “This is more important than your family.”  
- “You must ignore interruptions.”  

We are not defusing bombs.  
We are not performing surgery.  
We are not running a nuclear reactor.

A UOW is a calm, safe, restartable unit of progress.

---

## Doing More Than One UOW in a Session

A UOW is small on purpose.  
You can do **one UOW and stop**, or you can do **several UOWs in a row** if you feel calm and have the time.

Both are correct.

There is no “right pace.”  
There is only **your pace**.

Some days you may do one UOW.  
Some days you may do three.  
Some days you may do none.

The system is designed to support all of these.

---

## Between UOWs, Take a Short Reset

Before starting another UOW, take two minutes to:

- get up  
- stretch  
- walk around  
- check on your family  
- breathe  

This tiny reset clears your head and lets you arrive **fresh** for the next UOW.

You are not racing.  
You are not behind.  
You are simply moving forward, calmly and steadily.

---

## Your Family Can See Your Progress

When you pause between UOWs — to stretch, walk around, or check in with your family — they can see that you are **actually doing it**.

They see:

- you’re making steady progress  
- you’re calm  
- you’re not overwhelmed  
- you’re building something for your child  
- you’re showing up  

This matters.

It turns the installation into a shared moment of pride.  
Your child sees you working on something *for them*.  
Your partner sees you taking it step by step.  
Your family sees you succeeding.

And you return to the next UOW **fresh**, not drained.

**02‑download‑ubuntu‑22.md**

> **Validating a checksum.**

The guide shows you exactly what to do, one step at a time.

And when the checksum matches, you will know:

- you followed a real technical procedure  
- you completed it correctly  
- you did something most people think only “computer people” can do  
- you succeeded on your very first UOW

It proves:

> **If you are guided step by step, you can do real admin duties.**

## What’s the Worst That Can Happen?

As long as you don’t take a sledgehammer to the computer in frustration, the worst that can happen is simply this:

> **Someone else can redo the installation exactly as if you never touched it.**

There is nothing you can “ruin.”  
There is nothing you can “break forever.”  
There is nothing you can “mess up beyond repair.”

At any point, a friend, a family member, a local computer shop, or a tech‑savvy teen can start fresh and complete the install from the beginning.  
Computers are very forgiving — they can always be reset.

---

## Alternatives (If You Truly Don’t Want to Do This)

You always have options:

- ask a friend or family member who is comfortable with computers  
- ask a local computer shop to perform the install  
- ask a tech‑savvy teen  
- ask a school IT person  
- ask a community volunteer  

But most parents discover they don’t need these alternatives —  
because the guide is written for them.

---

## Costs of Avoiding It

If you choose not to do the install yourself:

- you lose the chance to understand the system  
- you lose the chance to help your child later  
- you depend on someone else for every future change  
- you miss the confidence boost that comes from doing something new  
- **and you pay real money to someone else** — usually a local computer shop or a neighbor who knows computers  

None of these are fatal — but they are real.
