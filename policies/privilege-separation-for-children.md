# Privilege Separation for Children  
A Household Policy for Safe Computer Use

This document explains why children receive standard (non‑administrator) accounts on household computers, even if they purchase the hardware themselves. The goal is to protect the system, protect the child’s learning environment, and maintain a safe, stable machine for schoolwork, creativity, and exploration.

---

## 1. Ownership Does Not Grant Administrative Privileges

A child may save money and purchase:

- a laptop  
- a desktop  
- a Raspberry Pi  
- or any other computer  

However, owning the hardware does **not** automatically grant administrative privileges.

Administrative access controls:

- system files  
- software installation  
- security settings  
- device configuration  
- system‑wide changes  

These are responsibilities reserved for adults.

This is similar to a child purchasing a car:  
They may own it, but they cannot legally drive it.

---

## 2. Why Children Receive Standard Accounts

Children receive standard accounts because:

- They are still learning how computers work.  
- They may experiment without understanding consequences.  
- They may follow online instructions without context.  
- They may accidentally break the system.  
- They may unintentionally remove important files.  

A standard account protects:

- the operating system  
- the child’s own work  
- the household’s data  
- the stability of the machine  

This is not a restriction.  
It is a safety measure.

---

## 3. Administrator Accounts Are for Adults Only

Administrator accounts can:

- install or remove software  
- change system settings  
- modify security policies  
- access all user data  
- repair or rebuild the system  

These actions require:

- judgment  
- experience  
- supervision  
- responsibility  

**Important:**  
Administrator privileges bypass all parental controls.  
For this reason, only adults in the household receive administrator accounts.

**Additional rule:**  
Children may not observe an adult typing the administrator password.  
This prevents accidental learning, memorization, or shoulder‑surfing.

---

## 4. Children May Participate in Supervised Administrative Tasks

Children may participate in administrative tasks **only under direct adult supervision**.

Examples include:

- installing the operating system  
- performing system updates  
- installing development tools  
- configuring hardware  
- learning how `sudo` works  

During supervised sessions:

- the child may type commands  
- the child must pause before pressing ENTER  
- an adult must confirm each action  
- the adult remains responsible for the outcome  

**Important:**  
During installation or any administrative setup,  
**the child may not provide the administrator password.**  
Only an adult may enter it.

This ensures the password remains private and prevents accidental privilege escalation.

This approach allows the child to learn safely without risking the system.

---

## 5. After Supervised Sessions, Administrative Access Is Removed

Once the supervised task is complete:

- the child returns to their standard account  
- the administrator password is not shared  
- the child cannot elevate privileges  
- the system remains protected  

This preserves the safety model while still allowing hands‑on learning.

---

## 6. The Goal: Safe Learning, Not Restriction

Privilege separation is not about limiting a child’s curiosity.  
It is about creating a safe environment where they can:

- explore  
- learn  
- build  
- experiment  
- make mistakes  
- grow as engineers  

Without the risk of damaging the system or losing their work.

---

## 7. Summary

- Children receive standard accounts.  
- Adults hold administrator accounts.  
- Ownership of hardware does not grant administrative privileges.  
- Administrator privileges bypass all parental controls.  
- Children may not observe adults typing the administrator password.  
- Children may not provide the administrator password during installation.  
- Children may participate in supervised administrative tasks.  
- Administrative access is removed after the session.  
- This policy protects the system and supports safe learning.

---

This document is part of the household computing policy and applies to all children using household or personally owned computers.
